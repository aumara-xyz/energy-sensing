## V3 VERSION NOTE — WHAT CHANGED FROM V2.1

V2.1 was already much stronger than the usual “GLP-1 fixes everything” narrative. It named negative trials, admitted the null, narrowed stage-dependency, and made the GHP-derived predictions visible. V3 hardens the document further for a skeptical medical reader.

The most important V3 change is that the framework is no longer presented mainly as a three-hub story. AMPK, mTORC1, and GLP-1R remain central, but V3 reframes the thesis as a **bioenergetic control-state problem**:

> Chronic disease may reflect loss of adaptive cellular control over growth, repair, inflammation, mitochondrial quality, and structural maintenance. Drug classes such as GLP-1R agonists, SGLT2 inhibitors, metformin, rapamycin/rapalogs, ketone-signaling interventions, and future tissue-directed platforms matter only insofar as they restore measurable control-state flexibility in the right tissue, at the right disease stage, with enough structural reserve left to convert signaling correction into function.

That shift matters. It makes the thesis testable in a way a clinician can attack. It also blocks the common failure mode: taking a positive cardiovascular or kidney outcome and rhetorically exporting it into Alzheimer’s disease, Parkinson’s disease, addiction, aging, cancer, or autoimmunity.

V3 adds nine hardening moves:

1. **Control-state model.** Disease is not “low AMPK” or “high mTOR.” The stronger claim is loss of adaptive dynamic range, phase coordination, and recovery after challenge.
2. **Structural reserve gate.** Stage-dependency is reframed as an architecture-vs-dynamics problem: if synapses, neurons, nephrons, hepatocyte architecture, or tissue scaffolds are already lost, pathway correction cannot be expected to rebuild them by itself.
3. **Target-engagement ladder.** Peripheral exposure is not CNS target engagement. Biomarker shifts are not clinical efficacy. Clinical benefit is not mechanism proof.
4. **Energy-Sensing Flexibility Index.** V3 proposes a measurable state-vector instead of a slogan: AMPK activation kinetics, mTORC1 suppression kinetics, ULK1 switch behavior, TFEB nuclear localization, autophagic flux, mitophagy, mitochondrial reserve, SASP burden, mPTP sensitivity, inflammatory tone, and recovery time after perturbation.
5. **Trial-bank reanalysis plan.** EVOKE, EVOKE Plus, ELAD, LixiPark, and Exenatide-PD3 become the first required falsification arena, not rhetorical evidence.
6. **Weight/glycemia null is promoted.** The null wins by default unless a trial or model separates direct control-state restoration from weight loss, glycemic improvement, survivor bias, healthy-user bias, and reverse causation.
7. **GHP is moved to provenance and hypothesis-generation.** GHP sharpens variables: topology, chirality, boundary, architecture/dynamics, and falsifiability discipline. It does **not** prove medical biology.
8. **The central R01-ready test is promoted.** The most fundable near-term experiment is the dynamic AMPK/mTOR/ULK1/autophagy challenge assay, not the broader disease-unification story.
9. **Topology predictions are demoted but preserved.** Protein-knot chirality, nuclear-lamina persistent homology, and chromatin-topology classification remain high-upside GHP-derived predictions, but V3 labels them exploratory until packaged inside mainstream biological questions.

The V3 claim is narrower, but harder to dismiss:

> The field should test whether chronic disease states are better classified by loss of adaptive energy-sensing flexibility plus structural-reserve thresholds than by diagnosis alone. If that control-state predicts response across tissues better than conventional labels, the framework deserves medical attention. If it does not, the framework weakens.

## ABSTRACT

Chronic cardiometabolic, renal, hepatic, neurodegenerative, addictive, inflammatory-aging, and progeroid conditions are usually managed in separate specialties. Yet many of them share disturbances in nutrient sensing, mitochondrial quality control, autophagy, inflammatory tone, and growth-repair balance. V2.1 framed this convergence through three hubs: AMPK, mTORC1, and GLP-1R. V3 keeps those hubs but hardens the thesis into a measurable control-state model.

The central proposal is that many chronic diseases involve a **loss of adaptive bioenergetic flexibility**: cells and tissues fail to shift cleanly from anabolic growth mode into repair, recycling, mitochondrial maintenance, and inflammatory restraint when challenged. AMPK, mTORC1, GLP-1R, SIRT1/3, PGC-1α, NRF2, FOXO, ULK1, TFEB, PINK1/Parkin mitophagy, ER-stress/UPR, inflammasome pathways, SASP signaling, and mitochondrial permeability transition are not separate decorations on the model. They are the actual state variables that must be measured.

Current clinical evidence is strongest in peripheral disease: semaglutide reduced major adverse cardiovascular events in SELECT, improved kidney outcomes in FLOW, and gained an FDA-approved MASH indication based on ESSENCE. Neurologic translation remains uncertain and stage-dependent: lixisenatide showed a phase 2 signal in early Parkinson’s disease, while Exenatide-PD3 was negative in a larger phase 3 trial; liraglutide in ELAD missed its primary Alzheimer’s endpoint but showed secondary signals; oral semaglutide failed EVOKE/EVOKE Plus on the primary Alzheimer’s clinical endpoint despite biomarker movement. Addiction signals are promising but not practice-changing: real-world semaglutide associations and a small phase 2 alcohol-use-disorder trial justify larger trials, not clinical adoption.

V3’s medical-facing thesis is therefore not that one pathway or one drug treats many diseases. It is that **response to bioenergetic intervention should be predictable by three measurable gates**:

1. **Control-state abnormality:** the disease tissue or accessible surrogate shows impaired challenge-response dynamics in energy sensing, autophagy, mitochondrial function, or inflammatory reset.
2. **Structural reserve:** the tissue still has enough architecture left for signaling correction to matter.
3. **Target engagement:** the intervention demonstrably reaches and modulates the relevant tissue at the relevant dose, schedule, and time.

If all three gates are satisfied and intervention still fails, the framework weakens. If response appears without control-state abnormality or without target engagement, the framework weakens. If weight loss and glycemic improvement fully explain outcomes under well-controlled causal designs, the null wins.

This document proposes a falsifiable translational program: a dynamic cellular challenge assay, retrospective trial-bank reanalysis, prospective stage-enriched trials, weight-clamped and pair-fed mechanistic studies, CNS target-engagement studies, and exploratory GHP-derived topology tests. It is a research program, not medical advice.

## PART 0 — THE ONE-PAGE MEDICAL-FACING VERSION

A clinician should not be asked to accept “unified chronic disease” or “energy sensing” as a worldview. The clean medical pitch is this:

> **Hypothesis:** Across several chronic diseases, treatment response to metabolic medicines is governed less by diagnostic label than by a measurable control state: dynamic energy-sensing flexibility, structural reserve, and tissue target engagement.

The thesis makes three claims and only three claims.

**Claim 1 — Peripheral disease already shows clinically meaningful outcomes.** GLP-1R-based therapies have hard-outcome evidence in cardiovascular disease and diabetic kidney disease, and semaglutide now has a MASH indication. These outcomes do not prove a universal mechanism, but they make the upstream energy-sensing question medically legitimate.

**Claim 2 — Neurologic disease is the stress test.** Alzheimer’s and Parkinson’s results are mixed to negative at phase 3. That does not kill the biological thesis, but it kills any simple class-wide repurposing narrative. The correct question is no longer “Do GLP-1 drugs treat AD or PD?” The correct question is “Which stage, which molecule, which tissue exposure, which target-engagement biomarker, and how much structural reserve?”

**Claim 3 — The falsifier must be built in before interpretation.** If early, biomarker-confirmed, structurally preserved, target-engaged patients do not respond, the framework weakens. If disease cells do not show impaired energy-sensing flexibility under controlled challenge, the framework weakens. If weight/glycemia fully mediate benefits, the framework weakens.

A medical reviewer does not need to buy GHP to take this seriously. GHP’s role is hypothesis generation and discipline: it contributed the architecture-vs-dynamics distinction, the boundary/control-state framing, the topology/chirality predictions, and the anti-self-sealing rule. The medical program must stand or fall on medical data.

## PART I — WHAT WE WERE NOT ASKING HARD ENOUGH

V2.1 asked whether energy-sensing biology could unify signals across chronic disease. V3 asks the questions that make the thesis hard to dismiss.

### 1. What is the measured state, not the metaphor?

“Energy sensing failure” must become a state-vector. A serious version must specify what will be measured, in which tissue, under which challenge, with what time resolution, and with what disconfirming result.

Static p-AMPK or p-S6 levels are not enough. Autophagy cannot be inferred from LC3-II or p62 at one time point without flux logic. CNS exposure cannot be inferred from systemic dosing. Neurodegenerative target engagement cannot be inferred from weight loss.

### 2. What does the null predict?

The null does not say GLP-1R agonists do nothing. It says their broad benefits are explainable by weight loss, glycemia, blood pressure, sleep apnea, cardiovascular survival, socioeconomic confounding, medication adherence, and reverse causation. Any V3 experiment must be designed so that the null makes a different prediction from the control-state model.

### 3. What is the tissue of action?

For CVD, kidney, and liver disease, systemic exposure plausibly reaches disease-relevant tissue. For Alzheimer’s and Parkinson’s, the central question is not “drug in blood.” It is CNS target engagement: receptor access, downstream signaling, cell type, disease stage, and whether the right neural or glial compartment is affected.

### 4. What is the stage gate?

Stage-dependency becomes falsifiable only when stage is defined before analysis. V3 shifts from vague “early vs late” to **structural reserve**: MRI cortical thickness and hippocampal volume z-scores, tau/amyloid/ATN status, α-synuclein seed amplification in PD, neurofilament light where appropriate, peripheral SASP/mPTP burden, fibrosis stage, eGFR/albuminuria class, and tissue-specific function.

### 5. What would count as failure?

A framework that can narrate every negative trial as “too late,” “wrong drug,” “wrong dose,” or “wrong tissue” is not scientific. V3 therefore requires pre-declared kill conditions.

### 6. Is the pathway causal or compensatory?

Energy-sensing changes may be upstream drivers, downstream compensation, or both. V3 requires directionality tests: longitudinal preclinical cohorts, genetic instruments where credible, conditional knockouts, pair-fed animal models, and challenge-response assays that measure failure before overt tissue collapse.

### 7. What is the dose-schedule logic?

Continuous activation of AMPK or continuous suppression of mTOR is not automatically good. Biological control systems need tone, amplitude, pulse, refractory period, and tissue-specific windows. “More repair signaling” can become pathology.

### 8. Which diseases should not fit?

A credible framework must predict its weak zones. Cancer, autoimmune disease, psychiatric disorders beyond addiction, infectious sequelae, and primary genetic mitochondrial/lamin disorders each stress the model differently. V3 keeps them as boundary conditions rather than forcing them into the same narrative.

### 9. What is the shortest experiment that could make a skeptical physician pause?

Not a grand clinical trial. The shortest high-leverage experiment is a blinded, live-cell perturbation assay showing that disease and healthy cells differ in energy-sensing dynamic range, AMPK-mTOR anti-phase coordination, autophagic flux, mitochondrial reserve, and recovery time after challenge — and that candidate interventions restore the dynamic signature.

### 10. What does GHP add without overclaiming?

GHP adds four useful medical translations:

- **Architecture vs dynamics:** structural reserve must be separated from signaling modulation.
- **Boundary/control-state thinking:** cell and tissue states are finite, context-bounded control problems, not isolated pathway cartoons.
- **Topology/chirality:** disease may involve loss of structural invariants in protein folding, lamina geometry, chromatin organization, and network flow.
- **Falsifiability discipline:** biology sharpens variables; it does not prove cosmology.

## PART II — FIRST PRINCIPLES: THE CONTROL-STATE MODEL

Every living cell must continuously solve a constrained allocation problem.

It must decide how much energy and material to spend on:

- growth and protein synthesis,
- repair and recycling,
- mitochondrial quality control,
- inflammatory response,
- stress resistance,
- proliferation or senescence,
- tissue-level function.

The decision is not philosophical. It is implemented through coupled biochemical regulators: AMPK, mTORC1, insulin/IGF signaling, GLP-1R/cAMP/PKA pathways, SIRT/NAD biology, PGC-1α mitochondrial biogenesis, NRF2 antioxidant response, FOXO stress programs, ULK1 autophagy initiation, TFEB lysosomal biogenesis, PINK1/Parkin mitophagy, ER stress/UPR, inflammasomes, SASP signaling, and mitochondrial permeability transition.

A healthy system is not one where AMPK is always high or mTOR is always low. A healthy system has **adaptive flexibility**:

1. It detects energy stress.
2. It suppresses inappropriate anabolism.
3. It initiates recycling and organelle repair.
4. It limits inflammatory overshoot.
5. It restores baseline when the stress resolves.
6. It retains enough tissue architecture for the restored signal to improve function.

V3 therefore defines chronic bioenergetic failure as:

> **A persistent reduction in adaptive control-state flexibility, measurable as impaired amplitude, timing, coupling, flux, or recovery after metabolic, inflammatory, or proteostatic challenge.**

That definition is intentionally testable.

### Core control variables

**AMPK.** Energy-stress sensor responding to adenylate charge and related metabolic signals. It promotes catabolism, mitochondrial adaptation, and autophagy-linked repair. But indiscriminate activation is not automatically beneficial; genetic or tissue-specific overactivation can produce pathology.

**mTORC1.** Nutrient and growth-factor-sensitive anabolic regulator. It promotes protein synthesis and biomass and suppresses early autophagy and lysosomal programs. But mTORC1 is necessary for normal growth, immune function, synaptic function, and tissue maintenance; chronic suppression can be harmful.

**ULK1 / TFEB.** The autophagy and lysosomal execution layer. This is where the AMPK/mTOR cartoon becomes operational. ULK1 phosphorylation state and TFEB localization matter more than simple pathway labels.

**Mitochondrial reserve and mitophagy.** A cell can appear metabolically normal at baseline and fail under load. Oxygen-consumption reserve, membrane potential, ROS handling, PINK1/Parkin recruitment, and mPTP sensitivity are necessary readouts.

**Inflammatory reset.** NF-κB, NLRP3, IL-6, TNF-α, CRP, microglial activation, and SASP markers must be interpreted as part of the control state, not as separate downstream decorations.

**GLP-1R.** A systemic coordinator of appetite, glycemia, gut-brain signaling, neuroimmune tone, cardiovascular risk, kidney risk, and possibly reward. Its effect depends on molecule, route, receptor distribution, blood-brain access, indirect vagal/area-postrema/nodose mechanisms, and tissue context.

### The strongest minimal model

The strongest V3 model is not a pathway diagram. It is a phase-plane model:

- x-axis: anabolic/growth pressure, represented by mTORC1/insulin/IGF/protein-synthesis signals.
- y-axis: repair/catabolic response, represented by AMPK/ULK1/TFEB/autophagic flux/mitophagy.
- z-axis: inflammatory and senescence tone.
- boundary condition: structural reserve.
- input: metabolic, inflammatory, or proteostatic challenge.
- output: recovery to baseline or collapse into chronic disease attractor.

Disease is predicted to show one or more of the following:

- flattened AMPK activation after challenge,
- delayed or incomplete mTORC1 suppression,
- impaired ULK1 switch behavior,
- reduced autophagic flux despite apparent marker changes,
- impaired mitophagy,
- elevated baseline inflammatory tone,
- slow or absent recovery,
- hysteresis: the system does not return to its original state after challenge,
- structural reserve below the rescue threshold.

This framing is harder to dismiss because it does not require a clinician to accept “one pathway explains everything.” It asks whether disease tissues have measurable loss of adaptive control.

## PART III — EVIDENCE MAP BY DOMAIN

### A. Peripheral cardiometabolic disease: strongest clinical evidence

**Cardiovascular disease.** SELECT showed that semaglutide 2.4 mg reduced major adverse cardiovascular events in adults with overweight/obesity and established cardiovascular disease without diabetes. This is hard-outcome evidence. It does not prove the V3 control-state model, but it establishes that incretin-axis intervention can alter outcomes beyond glucose lowering alone.

**Kidney disease.** FLOW showed semaglutide reduced clinically important kidney outcomes and cardiovascular death in people with type 2 diabetes and chronic kidney disease. This is also hard-outcome evidence. But V3 treats any “direct kidney mechanism” claim cautiously because weight, glycemia, blood pressure, inflammation, albuminuria, and survival sit in the causal pathway.

**MASH.** Semaglutide gained an FDA-approved indication for noncirrhotic MASH with moderate-to-advanced fibrosis. This strengthens the peripheral tissue argument, especially because MASH is a structural-inflammatory-metabolic disease. But V3 does not export MASH success into CNS disease.

**SGLT2 inhibitors.** SGLT2 inhibitors can be framed as pharmacologic fasting or caloric-restriction mimetics because they induce glycosuria, lower insulin tone, promote fatty acid oxidation/ketogenesis, and intersect AMPK/SIRT1/mTOR biology. This supports the control-state model but remains compound- and tissue-specific.

**Interpretation.** Peripheral disease is where the framework is currently strongest. The medical claim should begin here, not with Alzheimer’s disease.

### B. Alzheimer’s disease: the credibility stress test

The Alzheimer’s field is where overclaiming would destroy the framework.

**ELAD.** Liraglutide in mild-to-moderate Alzheimer’s disease missed the primary cerebral-glucose-metabolism endpoint, while secondary measures suggested possible executive and MRI-volume signals. That is not a positive definitive trial. It is a mixed phase 2b result that justifies mechanistic questions.

**EVOKE / EVOKE Plus.** Oral semaglutide phase 3 trials in early symptomatic Alzheimer’s disease failed to meet the primary clinical endpoint. Biomarker movement does not rescue a failed primary clinical trial.

**V3 interpretation.** Alzheimer’s disease cannot be treated as “metabolic disease of the brain” in the loose popular sense. V3 requires:

- biomarker-confirmed disease biology,
- explicit structural-reserve stratification,
- CNS target-engagement evidence,
- separation of vascular/metabolic benefit from neurodegenerative benefit,
- predefined success/failure rules.

The right question is no longer whether GLP-1RAs “work in AD.” The right question is whether a subgroup defined by preserved architecture, measurable energy-sensing impairment, and target engagement responds differently than a structurally late group.

### C. Parkinson’s disease: early signal, later failure

**LixiPark.** Lixisenatide showed a phase 2 signal in early Parkinson’s disease using MDS-UPDRS Part III in the on-medication state at 12 months. It is suggestive, not definitive.

**Exenatide-PD3.** Weekly exenatide failed at phase 3 using MDS-UPDRS Part III off medication at 96 weeks. That failure must be central in any honest V3 account.

**V3 interpretation.** PD is a perfect test of the structural-reserve model. A serious reanalysis should examine:

- disease duration,
- baseline Hoehn & Yahr stage,
- MDS-UPDRS trajectory,
- DAT imaging where available,
- α-synuclein seed amplification status where available,
- neurofilament light,
- diabetes/obesity/metabolic subgroup,
- drug exposure and tolerability,
- weight change,
- whether the molecule plausibly reached relevant CNS compartments.

The framework predicts that early, target-engaged, metabolically impaired but structurally salvageable PD should differ from moderate disease with established circuit loss. If reanalysis does not show that pattern, V3 weakens.

### D. Addiction: promising but not practice-changing

Reward and metabolic circuitry overlap through hypothalamic, brainstem, VTA, NAc, satiety, stress, and salience pathways. Preclinical circuit work supports GLP-1 involvement in drug seeking, and real-world semaglutide data show reduced AUD incidence/recurrence associations. A small randomized semaglutide AUD trial showed reduced laboratory alcohol self-administration and some weekly consumption/craving measures.

V3’s interpretation is strict:

- addiction is a serious research lane,
- existing human randomized evidence is too small for clinical adoption,
- the framework should predict which addiction phenotypes respond: cue-driven reward, metabolic dysregulation, inflammatory tone, comorbid obesity/T2D, or specific impulsivity/satiety phenotypes,
- larger trials must distinguish reduced craving/reward salience from nonspecific nausea, reduced appetite, or reduced social drinking.

### E. Aging and progeroid biology: mechanistic anchor, not universal proof

HGPS provides a fast-forward model of structural maintenance failure. Son et al. showed that p300 nucleocytoplasmic shuttling links nutrient status, AMPK/PP2A regulation, raptor acetylation, mTORC1 activity, and autophagy; this pathway is perturbed by progerin, causing mTORC1 hyperactivation and defective autophagy.

This is a strong mechanistic anchor that energy-sensing dysregulation can be causally consequential in a progeroid condition. But it does not prove ordinary aging is HGPS, and it does not prove that GLP-1RAs, metformin, rapamycin, SGLT2 inhibitors, or ketone interventions will treat aging. It creates a testable bridge:

> Do wild-type diet-induced obesity, senescence, or age-related tissues show the same control-state failure, and can pair-fed or weight-clamped interventions restore it?

### F. Cancer: inversion zone

Cancer cannot be folded into the same “repair mode good, growth mode bad” story without careful inversion. mTORC1 can be oncogenic; AMPK can suppress anabolism but may also support survival under stress; autophagy can suppress tumor initiation but support established tumor survival. V3 therefore treats cancer as a boundary condition, not a clean inclusion.

The framework’s cancer rule:

> Cancer is not evidence for the unified energy-sensing thesis unless the model can specify when repair signaling suppresses transformation, when it supports tumor survival, and how tissue-specific dosing avoids harming normal repair.

### G. Autoimmune, infectious, and psychiatric conditions: immature corridors

Autoimmune disease has plausible AMPK/mTOR/T-cell differentiation biology and emerging GLP-1 anti-inflammatory signals, but clinical evidence remains too immature and weight-confounded for strong claims. Infectious sequelae such as long COVID and post-sepsis cognitive decline are plausible but not established. Psychiatric disorders beyond addiction are not yet well captured by the model; metabolic benefits in antipsychotic-treated patients do not prove direct psychiatric symptom benefit.

## PART IV — THE MEDICAL NULL HYPOTHESIS

A hardened framework must give the null its strongest form.

### Steelmanned null

All broad GLP-1RA and related metabolic-drug benefits can be explained without a unified bioenergetic control-state thesis:

1. **Weight loss.** Reduced adiposity lowers blood pressure, CRP, IL-6, TNF-α, sleep apnea, hepatic fat, kidney hyperfiltration stress, and vascular risk.
2. **Glycemic control.** Improved glycemia reduces microvascular injury and downstream organ damage.
3. **Blood pressure and hemodynamics.** Some benefits may be mediated by ordinary cardiovascular and renal risk reduction.
4. **Healthy-user bias.** Patients prescribed newer expensive drugs may have better access, adherence, and surveillance.
5. **Survivor bias.** Cardiovascular survival alters later disease incidence.
6. **Reverse causation.** Prodromal AD/PD may cause metabolic changes rather than be caused by them.
7. **Publication bias.** Positive early-phase and observational signals may be overrepresented.
8. **Nausea/appetite confounding.** Addiction and reward signals may partly reflect reduced appetite, nausea, or reduced consumption drive rather than specific reward-circuit correction.
9. **Structural-damage model.** Stage-dependency can be explained by irreversible tissue loss without invoking a shared upstream control failure.

### What would separate V3 from the null?

The framework must show at least one of the following under rigorous design:

- disease cells have reproducibly impaired energy-sensing flexibility under challenge after confounder matching;
- intervention restores the dynamic signature in vitro or in accessible tissue before clinical improvement;
- response is predicted by predeclared structural-reserve thresholds;
- target engagement in relevant tissue predicts response better than systemic exposure;
- benefits persist in weight-clamped, glycemia-controlled, or pair-fed designs;
- early disease responds while late disease fails according to predeclared thresholds, not post-hoc narration.

### How the null wins

The null wins if:

- dynamic control-state assays fail to distinguish disease from matched controls;
- weight/glycemia/adiposity fully mediate the clinical outcomes in rigorous causal models;
- target-engaged early disease does not respond;
- late disease responds as well as early disease without structural-reserve dependence;
- topology/chirality metrics add no predictive value over standard assays;
- all positive signals disappear in adequately powered randomized trials.

This is not defensive language. It is the framework’s credibility engine.

## PART V — STAGE-DEPENDENCY AS STRUCTURAL RESERVE

V2.1 correctly promoted stage-dependency from caveat to organizing principle. V3 tightens it into **structural reserve**.

The key distinction from GHP is architecture vs dynamics:

- **Dynamics:** signaling, inflammation, autophagy, mitochondrial state, metabolic response.
- **Architecture:** synapses, neurons, nephrons, glomeruli, hepatocyte organization, fibrosis scaffold, vascular structure, extracellular matrix, nuclear lamina geometry, chromatin organization.

A dynamic intervention can restore function only if enough architecture remains.

### Alzheimer’s structural-reserve anchors

V3 does not impose a universal MRI cutoff because that would be fake precision. Instead, it requires that each study predeclare standardized thresholds using its own atlas, scanner harmonization, and normative reference.

Recommended anchors:

- hippocampal volume z-score or percentile,
- cortical thickness in AD-vulnerable regions,
- ATN biomarker status,
- amyloid/tau burden,
- tau-PET Braak-like stage as exploratory or retrospective unless acquisition/ROI definitions are standardized,
- neurofilament light as injury/degeneration context,
- baseline CDR-SB and cognitive domain profile.

V3 rule: **MRI structural reserve is clinically deployable now. Tau-PET Braak staging is promising but should not be the sole prospective gate until staging definitions are harmonized.**

### Parkinson’s structural-reserve anchors

Recommended anchors:

- disease duration,
- Hoehn & Yahr stage,
- MDS-UPDRS trajectory,
- DAT imaging where available,
- α-synuclein seed amplification assay status and quantitative measures when available,
- neurofilament light,
- cognition and autonomic/non-motor burden,
- metabolic syndrome / diabetes / BMI stratification,
- dopaminergic medication state.

V3 rule: a PD metabolic intervention trial should not merely enroll “Parkinson’s disease.” It should define whether it is testing prodromal, early, moderate, or structurally advanced disease.

### Kidney, liver, and cardiovascular anchors

Peripheral diseases already have better staging infrastructure:

- CKD: eGFR, albuminuria, fibrosis where available, SGLT2i/finerenone/RAS background therapy.
- MASH: fibrosis stage, cirrhosis exclusion, histologic response, noninvasive fibrosis markers.
- CVD: established ASCVD, inflammatory markers, lipids, blood pressure, HFpEF status, body composition.

The question is not whether stage matters. It is whether the Energy Sensing Flexibility Index predicts response above and beyond standard staging.

### Peripheral mPTP/SASP anchors

Peripheral tissue may provide accessible mechanistic readouts:

- skin fibroblasts,
- muscle biopsy,
- circulating immune cells,
- adipose biopsy where appropriate,
- senescence markers such as p16INK4a/p21/SASP profile,
- mitochondrial calcium retention/mPTP sensitivity,
- oxygen-consumption reserve,
- autophagic flux.

These cannot be assumed to represent CNS tissue. They are surrogate mechanistic windows.

## PART VI — THE ENERGY-SENSING FLEXIBILITY INDEX

V3 proposes an explicitly measurable index. It should not be treated as validated. It is a scaffold for experiments.

### ESFI core components

**1. AMPK challenge response**

- p-AMPK Thr172 dynamics or live AMPK biosensor signal,
- amplitude after oligomycin, glucose withdrawal, 2-deoxyglucose, hypoxia/reoxygenation, or inflammatory challenge,
- time to peak,
- recovery slope.

**2. mTORC1 suppression and rebound**

- p-S6K, p-S6, p-4EBP1,
- lysosomal localization where feasible,
- suppression amplitude after stress,
- rebound timing after nutrient restoration.

**3. ULK1 switch behavior**

- AMPK-linked ULK1 activation marks,
- mTORC1-linked ULK1 inhibitory marks,
- ratio and timing under challenge.

**4. Autophagic flux**

- tandem fluorescent LC3 reporters where feasible,
- LC3-II turnover with lysosomal inhibition,
- p62 turnover interpreted dynamically,
- lysosomal acidification and degradative capacity.

Static LC3-II or p62 alone is not acceptable as a primary flux readout.

**5. TFEB and lysosomal biogenesis**

- TFEB nuclear translocation,
- CLEAR-network gene expression,
- lysosomal capacity.

**6. Mitochondrial reserve and mitophagy**

- oxygen-consumption reserve,
- ATP-linked respiration,
- membrane potential,
- ROS handling,
- PINK1/Parkin recruitment,
- mitophagy reporters,
- mitochondrial network fragmentation/fusion.

**7. mPTP sensitivity**

- calcium retention capacity,
- cyclophilin D-associated measures,
- stress-induced permeability transition.

**8. Inflammatory/SASP reset**

- IL-6, TNF-α, IL-1β, CRP where appropriate,
- NLRP3 activation,
- p16INK4a/p21,
- SASP proteomic panel,
- time to inflammatory resolution after stimulus.

**9. Structural-reserve layer**

- disease-specific MRI, PET, histologic, physiologic, and tissue-architecture markers.

### ESFI primary output

The most important number is not baseline expression. It is **dynamic recovery**:

> How far does the system move under challenge, how coordinated is the response, and how completely does it return to a healthy baseline?

V3 predicts chronic disease cells and tissues will show a disease-specific loss of dynamic range, phase coordination, or recovery — and that responders to bioenergetic intervention will show restoration of that dynamic signature before or alongside clinical improvement.

## PART VII — THE FIRST EXPERIMENT A MEDICAL REVIEWER SHOULD CARE ABOUT

### Priority 1: Dynamic AMPK/mTOR/ULK1/autophagy challenge assay

This is the shortest path to credibility.

**Design.** Blinded live-cell and fixed-time-course study comparing healthy control cells with disease-relevant cells:

- healthy fibroblasts,
- senescent fibroblasts,
- HGPS fibroblasts,
- type 2 diabetes / obesity-associated fibroblasts or adipose-derived cells,
- iPSC-derived neurons or glia where feasible,
- disease-specific cells depending on collaborator access.

**Perturbations.**

- glucose withdrawal,
- 2-deoxyglucose,
- oligomycin,
- amino-acid starvation and refeeding,
- low-dose inflammatory challenge,
- rescue with metformin/AMPK-oriented perturbation, rapamycin/rapalog, GLP-1R pathway where receptor is present, SGLT2i where biologically meaningful, ketone/BHB signaling, and combinations only after single-agent dynamics are mapped.

**Primary endpoints.**

- AMPK activation amplitude and time-to-peak,
- mTORC1 suppression amplitude and rebound,
- ULK1 switch ratio,
- autophagic flux,
- mitochondrial reserve,
- recovery time constant,
- phase-plane hysteresis.

**Secondary endpoints.**

- TFEB nuclear localization,
- mitophagy,
- SASP reset,
- mPTP sensitivity,
- spectral complexity of baseline dynamics,
- single-cell heterogeneity.

**Prediction.** Healthy cells show coordinated stress response and recovery. Disease/senescent/progeroid cells show flattened response, delayed coordination, impaired flux, elevated inflammatory baseline, or hysteresis. Candidate interventions improve the dynamic signature only in contexts where the relevant node is impaired and accessible.

**Disconfirming result.** Matched disease and healthy cells show equivalent dynamic range, phase coordination, flux, and recovery, and interventions do not differentially restore disease-cell dynamics.

**Why this matters.** If positive, this assay becomes a companion-diagnostic seed. If negative, the framework loses its mechanistic center.

## PART VIII — TRIAL-BANK REANALYSIS PLAN

Before launching new clinical trials, V3 should mine existing negative and mixed trials. The goal is not to rescue them. The goal is to test whether the proposed gates were present.

### A. EVOKE / EVOKE Plus reanalysis

Questions to predeclare:

- Did baseline MRI structural reserve predict outcome?
- Did amyloid/tau/ATN burden modify response?
- Did biomarker movement occur without clinical benefit because structure was already below threshold?
- Did diabetes, insulin resistance, BMI, inflammatory markers, or vascular risk modify response?
- Did weight change mediate any cognitive or biomarker effect?
- Was there evidence of CNS target engagement beyond systemic exposure and peripheral metabolic change?
- Did sex, APOE status, baseline CDR-SB, or disease duration modify response?

Disconfirming pattern: no response enrichment in structurally preserved, metabolically abnormal, biomarker-confirmed subgroups; biomarker movement remains clinically irrelevant.

### B. ELAD reanalysis

Questions:

- Were secondary MRI/cognitive signals concentrated in structurally preserved participants?
- Did cerebral glucose metabolism fail as primary endpoint because it was the wrong timescale, wrong region, or truly unchanged?
- Did liraglutide exposure and weight change explain the signal?
- Did baseline insulin resistance or inflammatory markers modify response?

Disconfirming pattern: secondary signals are not reproducible, not structurally enriched, or explained by confounding.

### C. LixiPark vs Exenatide-PD3 reanalysis

Questions:

- Is the difference best explained by disease stage, molecule, endpoint, CNS access, trial size, or chance?
- Does early disease duration or lower structural burden predict response?
- Are DAT, α-synuclein seed amplification, NfL, metabolic syndrome, or inflammatory status effect modifiers?
- Did weight loss, nausea, or medication-state differences influence motor scores?

Disconfirming pattern: no stage, exposure, or target-engagement explanation survives; the LixiPark signal does not replicate.

### D. Addiction trial reanalysis

Questions:

- Does response correlate with baseline BMI, insulin resistance, craving phenotype, cue-reactivity, impulsivity, or nausea/appetite change?
- Are reductions in lab self-administration separable from generalized consumption suppression?
- Does GLP-1R intervention affect cue salience or reward prediction error rather than simply reducing intake drive?

Disconfirming pattern: effects disappear in larger blinded trials or are fully explained by nonspecific appetite/nausea reduction.

## PART IX — PROSPECTIVE CLINICAL PROGRAM

### Trial 1: Stage-enriched metabolic intervention in early neurodegeneration

**Purpose.** Test whether structural reserve and target engagement predict response.

**Population.** Early biomarker-confirmed disease only. For AD, use ATN/amyloid/tau status plus standardized MRI structural-reserve threshold. For PD, use early disease with biomarker support where available, disease-duration limits, and prespecified motor/cognitive status.

**Arms.** A single agent or rational intervention with known safety profile. Do not launch combination therapy until single-agent target engagement is established.

**Required measures.**

- structural reserve,
- metabolic/inflammatory baseline,
- target engagement,
- weight and glycemia,
- drug exposure,
- adverse events,
- clinical outcome,
- mechanistic biomarker panel.

**Primary hypothesis.** Treatment effect is enriched in participants with preserved structural reserve and measurable control-state abnormality.

**Kill condition.** No enrichment in the prespecified subgroup, or no target engagement despite exposure.

### Trial 2: Weight-clamped or weight-matched mechanistic study

**Purpose.** Separate direct control-state effects from weight loss.

**Design options.**

- Pair-fed diet-induced-obesity animal model.
- Human trial with behavioral/dietary matched weight-loss comparator.
- Mediation analysis that treats weight as a mediator, not a nuisance covariate to casually adjust away.

**Primary hypothesis.** Intervention improves ESFI measures beyond matched weight/glycemia change.

**Kill condition.** ESFI and clinical signals track weight/glycemia only.

### Trial 3: CNS target-engagement platform study

**Purpose.** Establish whether candidate interventions reach and modulate CNS-relevant tissue.

**Design.** Small mechanistic study before efficacy trial.

**Possible readouts.**

- CSF pharmacokinetics where ethical and feasible,
- neuronal/glial extracellular vesicle markers,
- PET ligand if available,
- inflammatory/microglial imaging where appropriate,
- neurophysiology,
- predefined downstream signaling markers.

**Kill condition.** No CNS target engagement at tolerable exposure.

## PART X — DRUG AND PLATFORM LOGIC

V3 is not a drug-stacking argument. It is a target-engagement and control-state argument.

### GLP-1R agonists and incretin agents

GLP-1R agonists are clinically established for diabetes and obesity and now have major outcome indications in cardiovascular and kidney/liver corridors. They differ in structure, receptor kinetics, route, half-life, tolerability, CNS access, and indirect neural engagement.

Key V3 rule:

> GLP-1R class membership is not enough. Each molecule needs its own target-engagement profile.

Oral small-molecule GLP-1 agents such as orforglipron may change access, adherence, and pharmacology. That does not automatically mean better CNS disease efficacy. It means tissue-distribution and target-engagement studies become more important.

### SGLT2 inhibitors

SGLT2 inhibitors are relevant because they induce a fasting-like systemic state and have hard cardiovascular/renal outcome evidence in their approved domains. For V3 they are not “AMPK drugs.” They are systemic energy-balance perturbation tools with downstream AMPK/SIRT1/mTOR/HIF/ketone effects.

### Metformin

Metformin remains a useful AMPK-oriented and mitochondrial-complex-I-linked comparator, but its clinical aging and neurodegeneration claims remain unproven. CNS exposure exists; the issue is effect size and target engagement, not absolute absence of access.

### Rapamycin/rapalogs

Rapamycin is a powerful mTOR tool and a major geroscience reference compound. Translation is constrained by schedule, immune effects, tissue specificity, mTORC1 vs mTORC2, metabolic consequences, and indication-specific risk. V3 does not support casual off-label longevity use or combination therapy.

### Ketone / BHB signaling

Ketone bodies and fasting-related signals are mechanistically relevant because they intersect energy state, inflammation, histone modification, mitochondrial use, and nutrient-sensing pathways. Clinical translation is less standardized and should be treated as mechanistic, not proven disease therapy.

### Combinations

Combination therapy is plausible only after state mapping. A rational combination should be justified by measured defects:

- impaired AMPK activation,
- stuck mTORC1 rebound,
- poor autophagic flux,
- mitochondrial reserve failure,
- inflammatory reset failure,
- structural reserve still present.

Without that, combination therapy is pathway enthusiasm.

## PART XI — GHP CROSS-REFERENCE: WHAT IT ADDS AND WHAT IT DOES NOT

This section is for internal provenance and hypothesis generation. It should not be the opening pitch to a medical reviewer.

### The safe GHP sentence

> GHP is used here as a variable-finding framework: it suggests that living disease states should be analyzed through boundary, architecture/dynamics, topology, chirality, and finite-capacity control. It does not prove the medical thesis.

### GHP translation map

| GHP object | Medical translation | Use in V3 | Non-claim |
|---|---|---|---|
| Architecture vs dynamics | Structural reserve vs signaling modulation | Defines stage-dependency | Does not prove clinical response |
| Observer-boundary / finite patch | Cell/tissue as bounded control system | Motivates ESFI and target engagement | Does not make consciousness a medical variable |
| Topological invariants | Protein knots, nuclear lamina geometry, chromatin architecture, network flow | Generates exploratory predictions | Does not prove disease causation |
| Chirality / braid memory | Composite-knot handedness and structural stability | Protein-knot prediction | Not clinical evidence yet |
| Anti-self-sealing discipline | Predeclared kill conditions | Governs trial interpretation | No post-hoc rescue of failures |
| Biology sharpens variables | Medical data inform GHP-scale hypotheses | Keeps provenance honest | Biology does not prove cosmology |

### Why GHP helps

GHP’s strongest contribution is not a medical mechanism. It is **question selection**.

Standard bioenergetics literature naturally asks about pathways, biomarkers, and drug classes. GHP pushes three additional questions:

1. **What structure must remain for dynamics to matter?** This becomes structural reserve.
2. **Which invariants define loss of biological organization?** This becomes topology/chirality/lamina/chromatin predictions.
3. **What would make the framework fail?** This becomes predeclared kill criteria.

### Where GHP must stay out

GHP should not be used to claim:

- that Fibonacci or topology proves AMPK/mTOR/GLP-1 biology,
- that medical trial outcomes validate cosmology,
- that chronic disease is one disease,
- that consciousness or observer-boundary physics is a clinical variable,
- that software or symbolic correspondence is medical evidence,
- that negative clinical trials are secretly positive.

The medical framework must survive without a GHP appeal.

## PART XII — REVISED FALSIFIABLE PREDICTIONS

### Prediction 1 — Disease cells lose adaptive energy-sensing flexibility under challenge

**Status:** Central, R01-ready.

Disease, senescent, or progeroid cells should show impaired dynamic response to metabolic challenge: reduced AMPK activation amplitude, delayed mTORC1 suppression, abnormal ULK1 switch behavior, impaired autophagic flux, lower mitochondrial reserve, slower recovery, or hysteresis.

**Disconfirming result:** matched disease and control cells are dynamically indistinguishable under well-powered blinded challenge assays.

### Prediction 2 — Structural reserve predicts response better than diagnosis alone

**Status:** Clinical translational priority.

In neurodegeneration and organ disease, treatment response to bioenergetic intervention should be enriched in participants with preserved structural reserve and measurable control-state abnormality.

**Disconfirming result:** response does not vary by predeclared structural-reserve thresholds, or late-stage participants respond equally well without target-engagement differences.

### Prediction 3 — Target engagement predicts clinical response better than systemic exposure

**Status:** Platform-gated, essential for CNS claims.

For CNS disease, systemic drug exposure should be insufficient. CNS-relevant target engagement should predict biomarker and clinical response.

**Disconfirming result:** target engagement is absent at tolerable doses, or target engagement occurs without any mechanistic or clinical signal in early structurally preserved disease.

### Prediction 4 — Weight-clamped designs preserve a residual control-state effect

**Status:** Required to beat the null.

A component of intervention effect should remain after weight/glycemia are controlled by pair-feeding, matched weight-loss comparators, or causal mediation designs.

**Disconfirming result:** all mechanistic and clinical benefits track weight/glycemia only.

### Prediction 5 — HGPS/progeroid axis generalizes partially to wild-type metabolic aging

**Status:** Mechanistic bridge, not proven.

Diet-induced obesity, senescence, or aged wild-type tissue should show partial recapitulation of p300/mTORC1/autophagy dysregulation, and interventions should correct it under pair-fed controls.

**Disconfirming result:** Son-axis dysregulation remains HGPS-specific and does not appear in wild-type aging/metabolic stress models.

### Prediction 6 — Topological nuclear-lamina metrics classify progeroid cellular state

**Status:** Exploratory GHP-derived prediction.

High-resolution imaging plus persistent homology should distinguish HGPS/progeroid nuclear architecture from healthy controls and correlate with autophagic flux, p300 localization, and SASP burden.

**Disconfirming result:** topology metrics add no classification or mechanistic value beyond conventional nuclear morphology.

### Prediction 7 — Chromatin topology adds disease-state information beyond standard epigenomics

**Status:** Exploratory computational prediction.

Persistent homology and topology-aware features on Hi-C/Micro-C data should improve classification of cancer, aging, senescence, or neurodegenerative cell states beyond standard compartments/TAD/loop features.

**Disconfirming result:** topology features add no predictive value in blinded held-out datasets.

### Prediction 8 — Composite-knot chirality affects protein mechanical stability

**Status:** Exploratory in silico / biophysical prediction.

Opposite-chirality composite knots should differ in mechanical stability, unfolding/refolding, and crowding response compared with same-chirality composites.

**Disconfirming result:** no measurable chirality-dependent stability or refolding difference across controlled MD or single-molecule assays.

### Prediction 9 — Neural reciprocal-flow loss is a systems-level readout, not primary proof

**Status:** Secondary systems-neuroscience prediction.

EEG/MEG directional-flow metrics in AD/MCI may show altered top-down/bottom-up balance and correlate with structural reserve and metabolic biomarkers.

**Disconfirming result:** directional-flow changes are absent, nonspecific, or unrelated to progression and metabolic state.

## PART XIII — STATISTICAL AND CAUSAL HARDENING

### 1. Predeclare DAGs

Every clinical claim should include a causal diagram specifying:

- treatment,
- weight change,
- glycemia,
- blood pressure,
- inflammation,
- sleep apnea,
- medication adherence,
- socioeconomic variables,
- baseline disease stage,
- target engagement,
- clinical outcome.

Weight is often a mediator, not a confounder. Adjusting for it naively can erase true effect or create misleading conclusions.

### 2. Predeclare effect modifiers

At minimum:

- disease stage,
- structural reserve,
- sex,
- age,
- baseline BMI/body composition,
- diabetes status,
- insulin resistance,
- kidney function,
- inflammation,
- medication background,
- APOE for AD,
- α-synuclein biomarker status for PD where available.

### 3. Separate mechanism tiers

V3 uses four tiers:

1. **Exposure:** drug is in blood.
2. **Target engagement:** drug modulates intended tissue/cell pathway.
3. **Mechanistic correction:** ESFI or relevant dynamic state improves.
4. **Clinical benefit:** patient-centered outcome improves.

A positive lower tier does not guarantee a positive higher tier.

### 4. Require negative-space reporting

Every trial or experiment should report:

- where no effect was found,
- where biomarker movement failed to produce function,
- where target engagement failed,
- where adverse effects limited dosing,
- where subgroup signals were absent.

### 5. Do not overfit topology

Topology and persistent homology are high-dimensional tools. V3 requires held-out validation, permutation testing, comparison against simpler morphology/epigenomic features, and no post-hoc feature fishing.

## PART XIV — SAFETY AND CLINICAL NON-CLAIMS

This document is not treatment advice.

The interventions discussed here have real risks:

- GLP-1RAs: GI adverse effects, gallbladder disease risk, dehydration risk, lean-mass concerns, rare pancreatitis concern, medication interactions, contraindication contexts, pregnancy considerations, and tolerability limits.
- SGLT2 inhibitors: euglycemic ketoacidosis, genital infections, volume depletion, perioperative management issues.
- Metformin: renal dosing, lactic acidosis context, GI effects, B12 deficiency.
- Rapamycin/rapalogs: immunologic, metabolic, wound-healing, mucosal, lipid, infection, dosing, and mTORC2 concerns.
- Ketogenic/fasting interventions: hypoglycemia risk in medication users, lipid effects, kidney stone risk, adherence, eating-disorder risk.

No V3 claim supports unsupervised off-label self-experimentation or drug stacking.

## PART XV — KILL CONDITIONS

The framework should be demoted if any of the following occur:

1. Dynamic challenge assays show no reproducible disease-control difference.
2. ESFI fails test-retest reliability.
3. ESFI does not predict response above conventional diagnosis/stage.
4. Weight/glycemia/adiposity fully mediate the apparent cross-domain benefits in rigorous designs.
5. Early, structurally preserved, target-engaged neurodegenerative cohorts fail without subgroup signal.
6. CNS target engagement cannot be achieved safely for relevant agents.
7. Stage-dependency fails when stage is predeclared.
8. Topological metrics add no value over conventional morphology/omics.
9. The framework requires post-hoc reinterpretation of every negative result to survive.

If several of these occur, the correct conclusion is not “we need a better metaphor.” The correct conclusion is that the framework is wrong or too broad.

## PART XVI — WHAT WOULD MAKE THE FIELD TAKE A SECOND LOOK

A serious medical audience will not be moved by a unifying story. It will be moved by a clean asymmetric bet.

The strongest asymmetric bet is:

> Build the dynamic AMPK/mTOR/ULK1/autophagy challenge assay, validate ESFI, and test whether ESFI plus structural reserve predicts response in existing trial datasets better than diagnosis alone.

The second strongest bet is:

> Reanalyze EVOKE/EVOKE Plus, ELAD, LixiPark, and Exenatide-PD3 under predeclared structural-reserve and target-engagement criteria. Publish the result even if it is negative.

The third strongest bet is:

> Run a small prospective mechanistic trial where clinical efficacy is secondary to target engagement and ESFI correction.

The fourth strongest bet is:

> Package the GHP-derived topology predictions as mainstream measurable biology: nuclear-lamina architecture in progeria/senescence, chromatin topology in disease-state classification, and protein-knot chirality in molecular stability.

The medical field does not need to accept GHP. It only needs to see that the framework generates precise experiments that standard repurposing literature would not have prioritized.

## PART XVII — FINAL SYNTHESIS

V3 is not a claim that chronic diseases are the same. It is not a claim that GLP-1R agonists, metformin, rapamycin, SGLT2 inhibitors, ketones, or future platforms are universal therapies. It is not a claim that positive peripheral outcomes prove neurologic efficacy. It is not a claim that GHP proves biology.

The strongest V3 thesis is this:

> Many chronic diseases may share a measurable failure mode: loss of adaptive bioenergetic control-state flexibility. Clinical response to metabolic intervention should require three gates: abnormal control state, preserved structural reserve, and tissue target engagement. Existing positive and negative trials are consistent with this possibility but do not prove it. The next step is not broader rhetoric; it is dynamic assays, trial-bank reanalysis, stage-gated prospective design, and predeclared failure conditions.

If those tests succeed, the significance is large: chronic disease classification would shift partly from organ labels toward measurable control-state failure and rescue capacity.

If those tests fail, V3 should be narrowed or abandoned.

That is the hardened version.

## APPENDIX A — COPY-PASTE MEDICAL ABSTRACT

Several chronic diseases may share a measurable failure of adaptive cellular energy control, but this does not mean they are identical or that one drug class treats them all. The proposed research program reframes AMPK, mTORC1, GLP-1R, autophagy, mitophagy, mitochondrial reserve, inflammation, SASP burden, and structural tissue reserve as a dynamic control-state system. The core hypothesis is that treatment response to metabolic interventions requires three gates: measurable control-state abnormality, preserved tissue architecture, and target engagement in the relevant tissue. This framing explains why peripheral outcomes in cardiovascular, kidney, and liver disease are stronger than current neurodegenerative outcomes, and why negative Alzheimer’s and Parkinson’s trials are central rather than inconvenient. The immediate test is a blinded dynamic challenge assay measuring AMPK/mTOR/ULK1/autophagy/mitochondrial recovery in disease versus control cells, followed by retrospective trial-bank reanalysis using predeclared structural-reserve and target-engagement criteria. If early, structurally preserved, target-engaged disease does not respond, or if weight/glycemia fully mediate benefits, the framework weakens. This is a falsifiable translational program, not treatment guidance.
