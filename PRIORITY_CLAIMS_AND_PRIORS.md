# Priority Claims and Priors

This note exists to make the public priors explicit.

## Highest-Confidence Priors

1. Chronic disease translation is heavily stage-dependent.
2. Structural reserve is likely as important as pathway modulation.
3. Peripheral success does not automatically port to CNS disease.
4. Biomarker movement is weaker than target engagement, and target engagement is weaker than clinical benefit.
5. Weight loss and glycemic improvement are serious null competitors and must be designed against directly.

## Strongest Research Program Claims

1. The field should measure **bioenergetic control-state flexibility** directly instead of inferring it from static pathway snapshots.
2. A dynamic challenge-response assay is the shortest serious test of the framework.
3. Chronic disease response may sort better by state variables than by diagnosis alone.
4. GHP contributes provenance, topology questions, and falsifiability discipline, but does not prove the medical thesis.

## Near-Term Predictions

1. Disease cells will show flattened or mistimed AMPK/mTOR/ULK1/autophagy dynamics under perturbation.
2. Structural-reserve markers will improve response prediction in neurodegenerative and fibrotic disease.
3. CNS programs will fail unless target engagement and preserved architecture are both present.
4. Some residual effect should remain in weight-clamped or weight-matched designs if the control-state model is partly right.

## Kill Conditions

1. Dynamic challenge assays fail to distinguish disease from control states.
2. Structural reserve adds no predictive value.
3. Target-engaged intervention still shows no meaningful signal in the right subgroup.
4. The null fully explains outcomes once causal confounders are handled well.

## Safe Summary

The point of this program is not to say "energy sensing explains everything." The point is to force a tighter, more measurable question about adaptive control-state failure and to let the data kill it if it deserves to die.
